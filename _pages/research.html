Morphology of the Multiphase Galactic Web kiloparsecs expand ↓

The interstellar medium exists in various phases - cold molecular clouds, warm neutral gas, hot ionized plasma (and more) interacting with their surroundings. The overlaps between specific phases and their morphologies have been hypothesized and found on 2D projections. We find similar phase-morphology correlations in 3D, namely the cold neutral medium (CNM, blue) overlap (red) with filamentary strucutres seen in the image above. Not all of the filamentary structures are CNM, however.

Working with researcher Chang-goo Kim and PhD candidate James Sunseri, I analyzed the spatial morphology of the multiphase ISM using high-resolution TIGRESS-NCR magnetohydrodynamic simulations and the morphology classifier PyCosmoMMF (Sunseri 2023). We characterized how different gas phases are distributed across the galactic "web" (analogous to the cosmic web on a local scale) and their correlations. We also studied the morphology structure of local dust map data (Edenhofer et al. 2023), which agrees with the TIGRESS-NCR mass-morphology distributions, but not volume-morphology distributions, potentially indicative of spatial compression.

Missing Galactic Center Red Giants parsecs expand ↓

Within a few tenths of a parsec of Sgr A*, the Milky Way's central supermassive black hole, bright red giant stars are far rarer than stellar population models predict. Several mechanisms have been proposed to explain the deficit, such as AGN feedback, stellar-black hole collisions, stellar-disk collisions, etc. Most proposed solutions are effective at simulating similar deficits much closer to Sgr A* (centiparsecs), although not all of them succeed at doing so further out. The image above describes the surviving distribution of stars at fixed semimajor axes divided by their final normalized angular momentum ($j$).

Under Professor Jeremy Goodman's supervision, I built Monte Carlo simulations tracking stellar evolution on the red giant branch alongside resonant and nonresonant two-body relaxation and stellar collisions. We find that tidal stripping by Sgr A* alone fails to explain the deficit, as loss cone diffusion rates grow only approximately logarithmically with stellar radius while red giant lifetimes shorten at a much faster rate. Instead, stellar collisions, amplified by the rapidly growing geometric cross section of evolving giants, seem more likely as an explanation. The simulations also predict an anisotropy in surviving red giant velocity distributions near Sgr A*, given smaller semimajor axes (see above).

Spectral & Interferometric Analysis of Post-RSG Yellow Hypergiants AU expand ↓

Post-red supergiant yellow hypergiants are among the most extreme evolved massive stars known. The fate of massive stars during the latest stages of their evolution is highly dependent on their mass-loss rate and geometry. The geometry of the mass-loss process can be inferred from the shape of the circumstellar material, which has a significant influence on the evolution of massive stars. In this context, post-red supergiants (post-RSGs) offer an excellent opportunity to study mass-loss events. The image above describes the L, M-band spectral profiles of both stars.

Under Dr. Evgenia Koumpia's supervision, I analyzed the L and M band spectral and interferometric data for IRAS 17163–3907 and IRC+10420. The analysis constrains the spatial structure, mass-loss rate, and geometry of the circumstellar envelope across multiple angular scales.

Dynamical Tides & Gravitational Waveforms of Neutron Stars kilometers expand ↓

Here is the introduction to my thesis for an overview of the topic. Binary neutron stars emit detectable gravitational waves close to merger, whose waveform encodes each star's internal structure, offering a direct probe of their interiors at a combination of density and temperature (currently) unreproducible on Earth.

In my senior thesis with Dr. Abhishek Hegade and Professor Sylvia Biscoveanu, I studied how dynamical, nonlinear tides modify the gravitational waveform during binary inspiral. We found that a nonlinear contribution to the tidal phase correction term ($p_2$) was found to enter at 8-PN order (in agreement with Pani et al. (2025)). Accurately modeling these tidal phase corrections is essential for inverting future LIGO/Virgo/KAGRA detections into constraints on the nuclear equation of state.

Classification of FQH Ground States nanometers expand ↓

The [fractional] quantum Hall (FQH) effect describes the [fractional] quantization of the Hall conductance induced by low temperature and strong magnetic fields. The image describes such FQH ground states on the disk, where the LSE measures the smallest deviation of the power sum symmetric polynomial basis from an orthogonal basis given the number of electrons (n). The non-orthogonality needs to be accounted for to extract physical properties (see overlap matrices).

I am currently investigating the classification of FQH states projected to the lowest Landau level (LLL) on various geometries by decomposing their wavefunctions into parton representations.
